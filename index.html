01 — Overview

One study, three workstreams

When a patient's hormone therapy is swapped between brands, does it change how they feel, how they cope, and whether they keep taking it? SWITCH brings together patients, healthcare professionals and national data to find out.

Main aim

To investigate the influence of adjuvant endocrine therapy (AET) brand changes in patients receiving treatment for breast cancer.

WS1

Patients & experiences

A 9-month observational study with embedded patient interviews.

WS2

Healthcare professionals

Exploring the perspectives of prescribers and pharmacy teams.

WS3

Brand changes & adherence

Examining excipients, adverse events and a retrospective CPRD analysis.

SR

Scoping review

Interventions that reduce nocebo effects in the oncology setting.

Patient experiences of brand changes

A 9-month observational study with embedded qualitative interviews, recruiting through NHS sites across the UK.

Recruitment against target 88%

Target raised to 650 patients (originally 500). 575 recruited and on follow-up, with around 88% of target reached.

Latest update

  • Recruitment began on 27 Feb 2025 and is due to finish 30 Nov 2025.
  • 29 sites open; 667 patients consented so far.
  • Around 70% survey completion rate during follow-up.
  • ~35 patients withdrawn; 92 consented patients have outstanding baseline questionnaires.
  • A separate local ethics application supports diversity recruitment — ~50 participants from minority ethnic backgrounds via community recruitment, with guaranteed incentives (£20 upfront, £20 on completing >60% of follow-up).
Aims
  • Determine how often brand changes occur in women receiving AET.
  • Examine the relationship between brand changes, adverse-event reporting and adherence.
  • Understand women's experiences through qualitative interviews.
Patient interviews

Starting ~January 2026, once participants begin their 9-month follow-up. Target ~30 women across diverse ages, backgrounds and side-effect severity. Mostly on Microsoft Teams or by phone, with in-person available locally in London. £20 voucher reimbursement.

  • Experience with hormone therapy so far, including early side effects.
  • Awareness of brand changes — were they informed in advance, and how?
  • Initial thoughts and feelings about the change.
  • Any change in side effects between brands, and how they were managed.
  • Worries about potential future brand changes.
  • Whether they ever requested a specific brand, their reasoning, and the response.
  • Problems accessing a requested or recommended brand, and the impact on adherence.
  • How support could be improved for affected patients.

Healthcare professional perspectives

An online HCP survey shared through healthcare networks, followed by focus groups to explore prescribing behaviour and views on brand changes.

180responses
~53%pharmacy
~30%nursing
~15%medical

Geographic spread: 76% England, 16% Scotland, 7% Wales, 2% Northern Ireland. The survey is now closed and data cleaning and analysis are underway, aiming to complete by the end of November. An abstract was accepted for oral presentation at UKIBCS in January, with a BPOS submission planned for March.

Key survey findings

  • Efficacy: 92.5% rated the same (4.5% lower, 3% higher).
  • Side effects: 81.3% rated the same profile (14.9% more, 3.7% fewer).
  • Safety: 94% rated the same profile (3.7% less, 2.2% more).
  • Fisher's tests were significant across all three (p<.001, p=.022, p=.037), suggesting differences between professions.
  • Efficacy: 88% rated the same (12% reported differences) — p=.031. Pharmacists more likely to report the same; doctors more likely to report differences.
  • Side effects: 57.9% rated the same profile (42.1% reported differences) — no significant professional difference (p=.133).
  • Safety: 83.5% rated the same profile (16.5% reported differences) — p=.024, pharmacists more likely to report the same.

Main drivers for an HCP-initiated brand switch were cost and availability.

Aim

Explore HCPs' prescribing behaviours and their experiences and perspectives on medication brand changes.

Focus groups — November 2025
  • Ethics approved; 13 HCPs confirmed so far.
  • 2–3 sessions on 25, 26 and 28 November.
  • Mainly pharmacists, with some doctors and nurses.

Focus: perceptions of different brands, patients requesting brands, practical barriers to switching, and ideas to improve practice.

03 — Workstream 3 · CPRD study

Aromatase inhibitors vs tamoxifen in routine care

Long-term treatment trajectories, effectiveness and safety — a CPRD analysis led by Chengsheng Ju, UCL School of Pharmacy. Move through the slides below.

Study aim

Long-term treatment trajectories, effectiveness and safety

Aromatase inhibitors (AI) versus tamoxifen (TAM) in routine care.

Chengsheng Ju · UCL School of Pharmacy

Design

A three-step analytical framework

  • Characterise longitudinal AET treatment trajectories over 10 years.
  • Estimate the effect of AI vs TAM on survival in postmenopausal women, benchmarked to the EBCTCG meta-analysis.
  • Extend the comparative analysis to the long-term benefit–risk profile of AI vs TAM.
Data sources

UK CPRD, 2010–2024

Linked with the National Cancer Registration and Analysis Service (NCRAS), Hospital Episode Statistics (HES) and the Office for National Statistics (ONS).

Tumour characteristics, anticancer treatment, AET from primary care, demographics and lifestyle, socioeconomic class, comorbidities, concurrent prescriptions, and complete follow-up to death and cause of death.

Population

Women with incident invasive breast cancer

Aged ≥18 with a first diagnosis between 2010 and 2018 who initiated an AET strategy. Step 1 is a descriptive drug-utilisation cohort; Steps 2 & 3 emulate a target trial in postmenopausal women aged ≥55.

  • Diagnoses from death certificates only.
  • Metastatic cancer or unknown stage.
  • History of other primary cancers (except non-melanoma skin).
  • Prior tamoxifen or AI use before diagnosis.
  • Under one year of continuous registration before diagnosis.
  • No surgical treatment for breast cancer.
  • Concurrent tamoxifen and AI, or multiple AIs.
Treatment strategies

The two arms being compared

Initiation of AI monotherapy within 365 days from surgery, and continuation of treatment.
vs
Initiation of TAM monotherapy within 365 days from surgery, and continuation of treatment.
Causal contrasts

How effectiveness and safety are estimated

  • Intention-to-treat: the effect of initiating AI vs TAM.
  • Per-protocol: the effect of sustained treatment with AI vs TAM for 10 years.
  • Patients are censored at discontinuation, unless there is evidence of disease recurrence. Switching between AI and TAM is allowed.
The open question

Extended AI vs extended TAM

  • 5-year AI vs 5-year TAM — AI better.
  • 7–10-year AI vs 5-year AI — unclear benefit.
  • 10-year TAM vs 5-year TAM — extended TAM better.
  • 10-year AI vs 10-year TAM — the gap this study addresses.
Benchmarking

Validated against the EBCTCG trials

The observational analysis was benchmarked to the EBCTCG randomised-trial evidence to test external and internal validity.

p = 0.56Overall survival — observational vs trials
p = 0.58Breast-cancer survival — observational vs trials

High p-values indicate no meaningful divergence from the trial benchmark.

Safety outcomes

Tracking the risks that matter

MACE — major adverse cardiovascular events MOF — major osteoporotic fracture VTE — venous thromboembolism

Assessed under both intention-to-treat and per-protocol analyses.

Key findings

What the data show

  • Real-world AET treatment trajectories are now characterised.
  • Benchmarking supports the external validity of trial efficacy in routine practice — and the internal validity of the observational analysis.
  • The benefit of extended AI vs TAM is less clear beyond 8 years.
  • Fracture risk increases with AI and continues to rise with extended AI; VTE is lower with AI; no clear difference in MACE.
Limitations

Reading the results with care

  • No information on intended treatment duration — end of planned treatment versus poor adherence is unknown.
  • No dispensation or consumption data; menopause status not directly recorded.
  • Residual confounding still threatens causal interpretation, with more assumptions required for the per-protocol analysis.
